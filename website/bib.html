Annotated Bibliography

Resources used for the project

  • Reddy, Rami, et al. "Potential impact of climate change on honeybees (Apis spp.) and their pollination services." Pest Management in Horticultural Ecosystem, vol. 18, no. 2, 2012, pp. 121-27.

    • This review article details research conducted and compiled by Rami Reddy, Abraham Verghese, and Varun Rajan concerning the honeybee population decline and its link to climate change. It initially addresses the global decline of various honeybee species, providing a table as a clear visual of population loss in the last 10 to 20 years across multiple countries. The major reason attributed to this loss—and the major focus of the article—is the consequences of increased temperature on pollinator-plant interactions. Essentially, the rapid temperature changes in recent years may affect not only honey bee physiology and behavior, but the production of pollen in necessary plants, impeding on their ability to maintain a healthy hive. The article then discusses the societal consequences of the issue, primarily focusing on the failure to pollinate popular crops, which directly affects humanity’s food supply. The final section is dedicated to proposing possible solutions to the problem, such as restructuring farming areas to better improve honeybee responses to changing climates.

    This source provides a general overview of the honeybee population decline, identifying specific consequences of temperature shifts on bee physiology, behavior, and interactions with their respective ecosystems. Compared to other resources, it offers a reliable perspective on a more global scale, intending to publicize the severity of the issue in terms of its international influence. The information in this source will primarily serve to provide more insight into the project’s focus, adding more details and possible solutions that will be presented on the final product.

  • Koh, Insu, et al. "Modeling the status, trends, and impacts of wild bee abundance in the United States." Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, vol. 113, no. 1, 5 Jan. 2016, pp. 140-45, doi:10.1073/pnas.1517685113.

    • In this paper, multiple researchers gathered data on wild bee abundance and uncertainty in the United States by utilizing a spatial habitat model, land-cover data, and quantified input from experts in ecology and biology. Results from this data collection have revealed that modeled bee abundance has decreased in many regions of the United States, with 139 counties having a mismatch between supply, or bee abundance, and demand, or the cultivated area intended to be pollinated. Additionally, crops highly dependent on pollinators have been demonstrated to retain a severe mismatch with the increasing demand and decreasing supply. The credibility of the issue is also cemented by the clear correlation between low bee abundance and its low uncertainty, leaving many researchers certain that the decline is facilitated by the degradation of pollinator habitats. If this trend should continue, agricultural costs in the US may increase, and can possibly result in the destabilization of crop production altogether.

    This source offers a quantitative and narrowed perspective on the issue of honey bee population decline, primarily focusing on the problem within the United States. Moreover, it emphasizes another cause of the decreasing population—habitat loss, often due to land being overturned for additional farmland and commercial establishments. Its information and models will add to the project’s statistical evidence, in addition to its restricted scope compared to the source above.

  • Schleeter, Ryan. Save the Bees, GreenPeace, www.greenpeace.org/usa/sustainable-agriculture/save-the-bees/#:~:text=Typically%2C%20a%20bee%20hive%20or,percent%2C%20in%20some%20cases%20more.

    • This source is a web page maintained by an organization dedicated to raising awareness on environmental issues while also providing a medium for collaborative groups to contribute to a solution. While the website features an assortment of issues, research, and volunteering activities, this webpage focuses on summarizing the main contributors to the honeybee population decline in the United States—particularly climate change and pesticides.. Additionally, similar to the first source, it offers solutions to the issue by discussing policies enacted in other countries to combat the issue. The website also features links to related articles and research documents that elaborate on the topic, as well as petitions, volunteering projects, and donations to relevant organizations.

    This source, while containing information relevant to the project topic, is not a useful source to rely on for evidence compared to scholarly articles or research papers. The source attempts to provide an objective view on the problem; however, its primary role is to incite engagement rather than educate readers on details of the problem or why the issue is specifically occurring. Rather than using this source for information or evidence, it acts a model website in terms of what content to include and how to organize it.

  • Naug, Dhruba. "Nutritional stress due to habitat loss may explain recent honeybee colony collapses." Biological Conservation, vol. 142, no. 10, 9 May 2009, pp. 2369-72, doi:10.1016/j.biocon.2009.04.007.

    • In this paper, Dhruba Naug of the Deparment of Biology at Colorado State University observes the correlation between recent land conversion trends and bee population declines. From Naug's study, he finds that recent changes in land cover for agricultural use have limited the amount of floral resources available for bees, restricting their colony development due to the lack of food. Naug also finds that bees deficient in energy due to limited availbility of nectar from habitat loss renders colonies vulnerable to pathogenic infection and lower temperatures. To demonstrate these losses, Naug plots trends of colony loss and honey development versus open land areas, revealing that open land areas are critical to colony health, which have been in decline primarily due to urbanization and agriculture.

    This source provides insight into habitat loss and its effect on pollinators; it details causes of habitat loss, how it specifically worsens colony stability, and also corresponds the data to beekeeper claims of colony losses due to poor foraging conditions. The study's information provides both statistical evidence of colony losses from unsuitable habitats, similar to a resource above, but also how the issue relates to recent observed trends and other pollinator stressors.

  • Kline, Olivia, and Neelendra K. Joshi. “Mitigating the Effects of Habitat Loss on Solitary Bees in Agricultural Ecosystems.” Agriculture, vol. 10, no. 4, Apr. 2020, p. 115. Crossref, https://doi.org/10.3390/agriculture10040115.

    • This article, by Olivia Kline and Neelendra K. Joshi, also observes trends in pollinator habitat loss, with a specific focus on solitary bees and other wild pollinators, primarily due to the prevalent research on social and domestic bees. Their study details how land with high agricultural intensity coincide with high rates of bee decline, and observes specific impacts on various species of wild bees. For example, mason and leafcutter bees use natural materials, such as mud and leaves, to construct their nests in the cavities of tree trunks and branches. Carpenter bees chew their own holes in wood to carve their own nests. This diversity of nesting preferences relies on diverse physical conditions within a habitat, which is eliminated when a land is converted to a more uniform site for farming. The study also describes how a reduction in biodiversity in habitats reduces the available of specific flora; for example, bumble bees have a preference for pollen with a higher protein content, meaning they are more likely to visit flowers bearing this kind of pollen. Finally, the paper offers recommendations for mitigating the issue and implications for the future should trends increase or decrease.

    This source also provides insight into habitat loss and its effect on pollinators; however, unlike the previous two papers, whih rely on quantitative data to reveal population trends, this study focuses more on how the issue specifically affects different species of bees. Additionally, it provides various policy recommendations and actions individuals can take to alleviate the crisis. It provides incredibly useful information for a more qualitative perspective, and also acts as a great source for possible solutions.

  • Goulson, Dave, et al. "Bee declines driven by combined stress from parasites, pesticides, and lack of flowers." Science, vol. 347, no. 6629, 26 Feb. 2015, doi:10.1126/science.1255957.

    • This study conducted by Dave Goulson, Elizabeth Nicholls, Cristina Botías, and Ellen L. Rotheray observes bee population trends in relation to various stressors, such as climate change, pathogenic infections, and increased pesticide use for agricultural management. It becomes clear from their study that stressors do not work in isolation, but often exacerbate the effects of one another; for example, increased insecticide use, particularly neonicotinoids, interferes with bees' immune systems at a sublethal level, and thus renders them vulnerable to pathogens they otherwise would have resistances to. The article also discusses possible solutions to the issue, such as encouraging the growth of bee-friendly flowers, improving habitat preservation policies, and a return to the principles of integrated pest management to avoid excessive pesticide use.

    This study is a flexible source that provides information for all three causes of decline covered on this site, with a specific focus on how all causes are related, working in tandem to accelerate population declines. While much of this information applies to all pages on this website, the majority of the article covers pesticide use and pathogenic infections; thus, the pesticide page primarily references the information of this article.

  • Sánchez-Bayo, Francisco, et al. "Are bee diseases linked to pesticides? — A brief review." Environment International, vol. 89, 2016, pp. 7-11, doi:10.1016/j.envint.2016.01.009.

    • This study conducted by Francisco Sánchez-Bayo and numerous other researchers focuses on the effect of pesticides, particularly neonicotinoids, on bee health. Since neonicotinoids are applied to seeds, it gradually spreads throughout the entire flower, including its pollen content, making it incredibly easy for it to enter a bee's digestive tract. It is found that neonicotinoids have both lethal and sublethal effects on bees' biological processes. If bees are not killed directly by the neurotoxin, their cognitive abilities and overall foraging performance are impaired at the sublethal level. Additionally, herbicides used to maximize available resources for crops risks reducing the diversity of bee food resources. The study also reveals that pesticides are a key contributing factor to an increase in parastic infections, due to an increase in pesticide use in recent years.

    This source also covers the effect of pesticide use on bees, similar to the source above; however, it also details the effect of neonicotinoids on bees themselves, rather than only observing changes in immune responses. The article essentially reinforces the information on pesticide use provided in the source above due to overlapping content, but its information on individual bee health is also integrated into the pesticide page.

  • Goulson, Dave, et al. "Decline and Conservation of Bumble Bees." Annual Review of Entomology, vol. 53, 2016, pp. 191-208, doi:10.1146/annurev.ento.53.103106.093454.

    • This article observes declines in bumble bee populations in both Europe and the United States, focusing on habitat loss, declines in floral abundance, and reduction in biodiversity due to agricultural intensification. It also attributes losses in the United States due to introductions of nonnative parasites from Europe. From the study's conclusions, its authors recommend regulation of commercial bumble bee use to prevent pathogenic infections, and agricultural management changes to encourage floral diversity. The study also reveals social impacts of pollinator reduction, questioning both the stability of crops that rely on pollination and the agriculture industry as a whole.

    This source's information on habitat loss and biodiversity overlap with other scholarly articles, reinforcing their credibility; however, the section on both the economic and crop output of pollination prove useful for demonstrating social consequenecs should these declines continue.

  • Goulson, Dave, and Elizabeth Nicholls. "The canary in the coalmine; bee declines as an indicator of environmental health." Science Progress, vol. 99, 2016, pp. 312-26, doi:10.3184/003685016X14685000479908.

    • This article discusses the environmental implications of pollinator population declines. To summarize, angiosperms relying on pollinators may face extinction should decreases continue, while other insect species, such as moths, beetles, and butterflies, are facing similar declines in population from shared causes. This risks both environmental biodiversity as well as the integrity of biological processes in the natural world, for insects are responsible for multiple natural phenomenon, such as decomposition, pest control, and food for larger organisms.

    This source focuses more on how pollinator decline impacts environmental health, while the other sources on this list focus more on the socioeconomic impacts of population drops. This adds another perspective to the impacts page of the website, revealing that not only are humans at risk in a pollinator crisis, but the rest of the natural world as well.
