Social and Environmental Impacts

What Pollinator Declines Mean For Us

Environmental Impacts of Pollinator Decline

"If insects were to disappear, the environment would collapse into chaos." - E.O. Wilson

Many species of bees are an essential aspect of natural ecosystems and agricultural development due to their pollination services. As much as 80% of wild angiosperm species are pollinated by animals — the majority relying on various species of bees. Thus, not only are many species of flowers and plants at risk of extinction, but a reduction in floral biodiversity would mean their respective pollinators could potentially face extinction as well, due to the mutual dependence between pollinators and pollen producers. Additionally, a decline in bee populations has numerous other environmental implications regarding different insect species. Similar trends of decline has been observed in other insect species; for example, long-running declines of moth and butterfly species have been monitored in the UK, particularly those associated with farmland habitats. A recent study revealed that 76% of butterfly species in the UK declined in either abundance or occurrence in the past four decades, with moths and carabid beetles following similar trends. In the United States, “ubiquitous” declines were discovered in lowland California butterfly species between 1975 to 2009, with many studies observing pernicious effects of habitat loss and agricultural intensification on these species. Declines linked to neonicotinoid use have also been observed; for example, farmland butterflies in the UK have been declining at an accelerated rate, with trends correlating to an increased use in neonicotinoids. Thus, a decline in bee populations could be indicative of a decline in insect diversity and abundance due to similar trends in decline, which are mostly facilitated by human practices. Not only do they provide pollination services, they are respondible for a plethora of other natural phenomenon: decomposition, pest control, and food for larger organisms. The rapid loss of insect populations risks the biodiversity of natural ecosystems and the very integrity of numerous biological processes.

Social Impacts of Pollinator Decline

Crop production also relies on bee pollination; around 9.5% of total worldwide agricultural production encompasses pollination services. This contribution makes up an estimated 15-30% of the human diet, including both the direct pollination of produce consumed by humans, and the pollination of plants, such as alfalfa and clover, used to feed livestock. In the United States, pollination services contributed an estimated 11% of the nation’s agricultural gross domestic product in 2009, and estimated $3.07 billion annually comes from the plants pollinated by wild bees. While bee populations have been in decline, consumption of specialty fruit and vegetable crops, as well as the rate of growth for the human population, has grown exponentially. Pollinator demands have only increased as time progresses, and pollinators are unable to keep pace, risking the health of popular crops within the agriculture industry, as well as popular food sources in the human diet, such as almonds, cherries, blueberries, apples, watermelons, and squash. Crops such as mangoes, litchi, and coffee have periods of mass blooming over relatively short periods, raising the likelihood of pollinator mismatches due to human activity.
